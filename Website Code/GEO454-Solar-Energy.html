Background

Introduction

Switzerland’s electricity consumption in the year 2021 amounts to 57 TWh, of which roughly 80% can already be attributed to renewable energy sources. However, Switzerland’s electricity consumption is projected to grow to 84 TWh until the year 2050 due to various factors such as population growth, electrification of previously fossil-fuel powered industries as well as technological advancements such as the widespread adoption electric vehicles or artificial intelligence. This increased demand will not be able to be covered by nuclear or hydropower, since Switzerland plans a gradual phase-out of nuclear energy and since the potential of hydropower has largely been exhausted due to conflicting concerns regarding ecology and landscape protection. 

That is where solar energy comes into play. Compared to nuclear power, there are no concerns of devastating accidents or radioactive waste management and the impact of the plants on ecosystems and nature is much less significant as opposed to hydropower plants. As of 2021, solar energy currently accounts for just 5% of Switzerland’s annual electricity consumption, which means that Switzerland is (solar-)eclipsed in this regard by neighboring countries such as Germany or Italy. Even though the installed power of all photovoltaic plants exceeds the power of Switzerland’s four active nuclear power plants, there is still much room to grow. Studies show that the combined photovoltaic potential of the roofs and facades of buildings in Switzerland amounts to 85 TWh, which means that the predicted electricity consumption for 2050 could theoretically be fully covered by installing photovoltaic plants on all suitable roofs and facades in Switzerland. However, only roughly 4% of this potential is currently exhausted. 

Therefore, solar energy is the key to a successful transition towards renewable energy, having the potential to address a wide range of the Sustainable Development Goals outlined by the UN. Electricity harvested through the power of the Sun constitutes responsible production patterns in the form of sustainable infrastructure, which can provide affordable and clean energy, contributing to the sustainability of human settlements and thereby also to the mitigation of climate change.​​Switzerland 

Research Questions

In light of the identified importance of solar energy in the transition towards renewable energies, we seek to investigate the temporal and spatial development of the exhaustion of the photovoltaic potential of Switzerland’s roofs and facades by comparing the power of currently installed photovoltaic plants to the theoretical potential determined for each municipality by the Swiss Federal Office of Energy. Moreover, we also aim to examine possible correlations with socioeconomic factors that hinder or boost the development of solar energy adoption in Switzerland. By computing these metrics on the municipality-level and subsequently aggregating them to different administrative units such as cantons or municipality types, we also aim to combat the MAUP during this study. Therefore, we aim to investigate the following research questions and hypotheses. 

  • What are the spatial and temporal patterns of the solar energy potential exhaustion in Switzerland? 
  • Is the fraction of photovoltaic potential exhaustion higher in left-oriented municipalities or cantons?​  Is there a correlation with the political orientation?
  • Is the fraction of photovoltaic potential exhaustion higher in municipalities or cantons that display a larger fraction of home ownership?

Data & Methods

  • Electricity production plants: Contains the location, power in kW and commissioning date of photovoltaic plants in Switzerland, which are registered in the Swiss proof of origin system and includes all plants with an output exceeding 30 kilovolt-amperes (kVA), as well as small plants (more than 2 kilowatts) that have been voluntarily registered to display proof of origin. The dataset also includes plants that are subsidized by a feed-in rate, a one-off payment, additional cost financing or an investment contribution.
  • Solar energy potential: Contains the results of the study conducted by the Swiss Federal Office of Energy. The dataset shows the solar energy potential of the roofs and facades for every municipality in Switzerland in GWh for various scenarios with differing assumptions. We work with the scenario that assumes that all roofs and facades will be exclusively used for electricity production and not for heat production purposes.
  • Strength of political parties in municipal national council elections in 2019: Contains the results of national council elections for each municipality in Switzerland in terms of the fraction of obtained votes for each party.
  • Homeownership rate: Contains the homeownership rate for select cities, districts and cantons.
  • SwissBOUNDARIES3D: Contains the geometries of the municipalities and cantons.
  • Typology of municipalities: Contains the classification of municipalities in nine distinct typologies.

The fraction of exhaustion of photovoltaic potential for a municipality or canton is determined by computing the sum of the installed power of all plants within the respective boundaries, converting the power to an energy output by assuming a factor of 950 kWh per kW and subsequently dividing by the photovoltaic potential determined for each municipality or canton by the Swiss Federal Office of Energy.

The political orientation is determined by assigning a score to every Swiss political party based on whether they tend to be left- or right-wing. The political orientation of a municipality or canton is then computed as the weighted average between this score and the fraction of votes that the party managed to obtain in the respective municipality or canton. This yields a numerical score which indicates whether a municipality or cantons tends towards the left or right.
